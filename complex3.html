Step 1: New Technology as an Additional Option

Any new technology introduces an additional option in the environment for performing tasks that could already be accomplished through existing means.

Key Insight: Technology doesn't replace existing methods but adds to the set of available choices, increasing the overall complexity of the environment.

Consider communication: Before email, we had letters, telephones, and face-to-face meetings. Email became an additional option, not a replacement for all previous methods. This pattern repeats with each technological advancement.

Step 2: Individual & Environment Relationship

Individuals exist in relationship with their environment. They have less complexity than the environment and manage this through a two-way process:

Attenuation

Selecting and filtering signals from the environment to reduce complexity

Niche Creation

Generating many descriptions of the same thing to create a stable place to dwell

This creates a feedback loop: individuals attenuate environmental complexity by selecting relevant signals, and simultaneously create niches in the environment through their actions and descriptions. Niche creation is essentially about creating stability through multiple perspectives on the same reality.

Step 3: Rise in Technology Options

The proliferation of technology options increases the challenge of selection. With more options comes:

  • Increased uncertainty about which option to choose
  • Difficulty identifying criteria for selection between alternatives
  • Reduced ability to construct stable niches as technologies rapidly change

This creates a paradox: more options should mean more freedom, but instead they often lead to decision paralysis and increased cognitive load. The individual's attenuation mechanisms become overwhelmed by the sheer volume of technological signals.

Example: Consider project management tools. With dozens of options (Asana, Trello, Jira, Basecamp, Monday.com, etc.), selecting the "right" one becomes increasingly difficult, and the criteria for selection (cost, features, integration) become harder to define.

Step 4: Institutional Attenuation

To manage individual uncertainty, institutions create a second-layer attenuation system:

  • Bureaucracy - Formal procedures and hierarchies
  • Templates & Standards - Pre-defined ways of doing things
  • Rules & Policies - Constraints on choice and action
  • Certification & Approval - Gatekeeping mechanisms

This institutional layer "mops up" some of the user's uncertainty by reducing the available options and providing clear (if sometimes arbitrary) selection criteria. However, this attenuation creates its own complexity and may not align with the actual needs of individuals or the realities of the environment.

Example: A university's IT department might limit software options to only those that meet specific security and compatibility standards, reducing the burden on individual faculty members to evaluate every possible tool.

Step 5: Conflict for Users

Users find themselves squeezed between two worlds:

Personal Way

Direct contact with environment, intuitive, adaptive

Institutional Way

Mediated through bureaucracy, standardized, constrained

The institutionally attenuated world is not the real world, creating conflict for users as they navigate between their personal way (in direct contact with the environment) and the institutional way (mediated through rules and templates).

This tension creates cognitive dissonance and additional uncertainty as users must constantly decide whether to follow institutional protocols or trust their own judgment and experience.

Example: A doctor may know from experience that a particular treatment works best for certain patients, but hospital protocols might require a different, standardized approach. The doctor is squeezed between institutional requirements and professional judgment.

Step 6: Positive Feedback Loop

The tension between personal and institutional approaches drives further innovation:

  1. Users create workarounds and new tools to bridge the gap
  2. These innovations become new technologies
  3. New technologies increase environmental complexity
  4. Increased complexity creates more uncertainty
  5. Institutions respond with more attenuation
  6. The cycle repeats, accelerating over time

This creates a positive feedback loop where complexity begets more complexity, and attenuation mechanisms struggle to keep pace with the rate of innovation. The result is an ever-accelerating cycle of technological change and institutional adaptation.

Example: The proliferation of communication tools (Slack, Teams, Zoom, etc.) was a response to email overload. But now organizations have to manage multiple communication platforms, creating new complexity that requires new management approaches.
